Part 4 What to do

What to Do in Toronto, by Area and Era.

A mapped, area-organized guide to twenty-one things to do — leaning museums, history, and architecture, grouped so you can pair a couple of stops in one outing. No car needed; the TTC and a short Uber cover it.

Sources: the museums & sites themselves · City of Toronto · Atlas Obscura — hours and (dynamic) ticketing verified, but confirm before you go.

How to Use This

Pick the Area, Then the Day.

Toronto rewards doing a couple of nearby things in one outing rather than criss-crossing on the TTC. This section is organized by area for exactly that — and skews to museums, history, and architecture over the outdoors.

21
things to do across 7 areas
8
free — incl. the Distillery and Allan Gardens
3
one-area days that need zero Uber
1
flagged trek (the Aga Khan Museum)

The marquee museums cluster on Bloor (the ROM and the Gardiner are across the street from each other) and on the west side (the AGO anchors Chinatown/Kensington). The historic core near Union — St. Lawrence Market, the Distillery District, the civic architecture — is mostly free and entirely walkable, with the underground PATH as a weather-proof connector.

A few honest notes: the CN Tower is worth one clear-day visit and skippable otherwise; the Aga Khan Museum is spectacular but a real haul north; and the Toronto Islands ferry is here as a scenic skyline ride, not an athletic outing. Pick an area per outing and let the TTC do the rest.

The shape of it: one Bloor museum afternoon, one west-side AGO-and-Kensington day, one Old Town market-and-Distillery walk — with Casa Loma as the half-day castle. Skip the CN Tower restaurant and Ripley's.
1

One Bloor afternoon does Museum Row.

The ROM → the Gardiner (across Queen's Park) → the Bata Shoe Museum toward the Annex, capped with a Yorkville stroll. All Line 1/2 accessible; zero Uber.

2

One west-side day is the AGO and the markets.

The AGO (Gehry façade, the Thomson collection) → a Chinatown lunch → Kensington Market → Graffiti Alley. Entirely walkable, ends near Osgoode station.

3

The Old Town day is mostly free.

St. Lawrence Market (skip Mondays) → the Distillery District → the civic-architecture loop at Nathan Phillips Square and Osgoode Hall, with the Hockey Hall of Fame on the walk from Union.

4

Casa Loma is the half-day castle.

Casa Loma + Spadina House next door — one Uber up the hill covers both. The most on-brand history-and-architecture stop in the guide.

Bloor / Yorkville (Museum Row)

Bloor / Yorkville (Museum Row) · 3 stops.

The densest museum cluster in the city, walkable along Bloor between St. George and Museum stations. The ROM, the Gardiner across the street, and the Bata Shoe Museum toward the Annex — one full afternoon.

Museum $26-30

Royal Ontario Museum (ROM)

Canada's largest museum, fronted by Daniel Libeskind's shattered-glass Crystal jutting over Bloor Street.

Bloor / Yorkville Museum $26-30 The Crystal

The 2007 Libeskind addition — aluminum-and-glass prisms crashing into the 1914 Beaux-Arts building — is the most argued-about architecture in the city. Inside, go straight for the dinosaurs, the Bat Cave, and the Chinese and Egyptian galleries. Budget two to three hours.

Gardiner Museum

The only museum in Canada devoted entirely to ceramic art — small, calm, the perfect ROM counterweight.

Bloor / Yorkville Museum $15 Across from the ROM

A 2,900-piece collection from ancient American ceramics to Italian Renaissance maiolica to contemporary clay, directly across Queen's Park from the ROM. Intimate where the ROM is overwhelming — an hour is plenty.

Bata Shoe Museum

A whole museum about footwear — far better and stranger than that sounds.

Bloor / Yorkville Museum $14 In a Moriyama Box

Housed in a Raymond Moriyama building shaped like a shoebox with its lid askew, the collection runs from 4,500-year-old sandals to Elton John's platform boots. A genuinely good cultural-history museum that happens to use shoes as its lens. One hour.

Downtown / Old Town

Downtown / Old Town · 9 stops.

The historic commercial core near Union Station, exactly where you arrive. Mostly free, weather-proof, and walkable: the market, the Distillery, the civic architecture, and the underground PATH. One to two outings.

Market Free

St. Lawrence Market

A 200-year-old market hall National Geographic once named the world's best.

Old Town Market Free 200-Year-Old Market

The South Market sits in a building incorporating Toronto's 1845 second City Hall. Get a peameal bacon sandwich at Carousel Bakery, browse the cheese and seafood stalls, and time a Saturday for the North Market farmers' market. Ten minutes from Union.

Distillery District

Forty-plus red-brick Victorian distillery buildings, cobblestoned and pedestrian-only — now galleries, studios, and cafes.

Old Town Historic Free N. America's Best Victorian Industrial

The former Gooderham & Worts complex, once the largest distillery in the British Empire, is the best-preserved Victorian industrial architecture on the continent and entirely car-free. Wander the cobbled lanes and the galleries; spectacular at Christmas.

Hockey Hall of Fame

Canada's hockey shrine, set in a gorgeous 1885 Bank of Montreal that survived the 1904 fire.

Financial District Museum $25 Inside an 1885 Bank

Even non-fans should poke into the Great Hall — the former bank's domed, stained-glass banking room now holds the original Stanley Cup. The building itself, a Beaux-Arts bank at Yonge and Front, is the real architecture pick.

Nathan Phillips Square / City Hall

Viljo Revell's 1965 modernist masterpiece — two curved towers cradling a UFO-like council chamber.

Downtown Architecture Free The 3D TORONTO Sign

The Finnish architect's design beat 500 entries and remains one of the boldest civic buildings of its era. The square has the illuminated 3D TORONTO sign (great at night) and a reflecting pool; across the street, the Romanesque Old City Hall (1899) gives you two eras in one stop.

Osgoode Hall

A palatial 1830s legal building with a jaw-dropping Great Library — and famously absurd cow gates out front.

Downtown Historic Free The Cow-Gates

Home of the Law Society of Ontario, this Neoclassical pile hides one of the most beautiful interiors in Toronto: the Great Library, 40-foot ceilings, a spiral staircase. The wrought-iron gates were reportedly designed to keep cattle off the grounds.

Little Canada

Canada in miniature — astonishingly detailed model landscapes with a day-night cycle, below Yonge-Dundas.

Downtown Attraction $36 Quirky · Indoor

A 45,000-sq-ft world of HO-scale model towns — Toronto, Niagara, Quebec, the Rockies — with moving cars, trains, and a recurring sunrise-to-night light cycle. Genuinely impressive craft and a great rainy-afternoon move. Allow about two hours.

The honest take: the LookOut and Glass Floor deliver a genuinely great view of the city and lake, and the tower is the unavoidable skyline landmark. Go on a clear day or skip it. EdgeWalk is a real thrill for the right person; the 360 restaurant is overpriced.

Queen West / Chinatown / Kensington

Queen West / Chinatown / Kensington · 3 stops.

The bohemian, walkable west side. The AGO anchors it; Chinatown, Kensington Market, and Graffiti Alley are all a stroll. One Dundas/Queen West outing.

Museum $30

Art Gallery of Ontario (AGO)

A great encyclopedic art museum wrapped in Frank Gehry's swooping Douglas-fir-and-glass façade — in the neighbourhood he grew up in.

Chinatown / Dundas W Museum $30 The Gehry Façade

Gehry's 2008 transformation gave the AGO a 600-foot curving gallery along Dundas and a spiralling staircase that pierces the roof. Inside: the Thomson Collection, the Group of Seven landscapes, and a strong contemporary wing. Two to three hours.

Kensington Market

Toronto's most characterful district — vintage shops, painted Victorian houses, global food, no chains.

Kensington Walk Free The Bohemian Walk

A National Historic Site and the antidote to the polished downtown: narrow streets of fruit stalls, taco joints, cheese shops, and graffiti alleys. Just wander Augusta and Kensington Avenues; it flows straight into Chinatown for dumplings and produce.

Graffiti Alley (Rush Lane)

Three legalized, ever-changing blocks of murals behind Queen West — the best free art in the city.

Queen West Gallery Free Free Street-Art Mile

Rush Lane runs just south of Queen West between Spadina and Portland, every surface covered in sanctioned murals that turn over constantly, so it's never the same twice. A 15-minute self-guided walk; pair it with the Queen West boutiques.

Casa Loma Hill

Casa Loma Hill · 2 stops.

Two historic estates on the same hill, a short Uber up from St. Clair West or Dupont. Casa Loma the spectacle, Spadina House the intimacy — one outing.

Historic $30

Casa Loma

A full-blown 1914 Gothic Revival castle — 98 rooms, secret passages, towers, and stables — perched above the city.

Casa Loma Historic $30 Toronto's Castle

Financier Sir Henry Pellatt's folly is the most on-brand stop in this guide: turrets, an oak Great Hall, an 800-foot tunnel to the stables, and a skyline view. The included audio guide tells the rags-to-riches-to-ruin story. Two to three hours.

Spadina Museum

A meticulously restored 1920s gentleman's estate right beside Casa Loma — the quieter, human-scaled counterpart.

Casa Loma Historic Free Next to the Castle

Where Casa Loma is spectacle, Spadina House is intimacy: the Austin family's mansion frozen in the 1920s-30s with original furnishings, set in restored Edwardian gardens. Visiting the house is by guided tour, which is part of the charm.

Harbourfront / Waterfront

Harbourfront / Waterfront · 2 stops.

Where Union Station meets the lake. The Islands ferry is the marquee skyline outing (light, optional outdoors); Fort York is the War-of-1812 history nearby.

Landmark $9.57

Toronto Islands Ferry

A 15-minute ferry to a string of car-free islands — framed as a scenic skyline outing, not a workout.

Harbourfront Landmark $9.57 The Skyline Shot

Even if you never leave the dock, the ferry back gives the single best view of the skyline. On the islands, a flat stroll on Ward's Island (cottage lanes, a boardwalk, a cafe) needs no athletics. Easy walk from Union to the Jack Layton Terminal.

Fort York

The birthplace of Toronto — original 1813-era buildings on the site of the battle that razed the town.

Harbourfront Historic Free War of 1812

Canada's largest collection of original War of 1812 buildings sits, surreally, beneath the Gardiner Expressway. This is where American forces sacked York in 1813 (the act Canadians later avenged by burning Washington). Costumed guides and a sharp visitor centre.

Garden District

Garden District · 1 stop.

A historic Victorian glasshouse near downtown's east edge — Allan Gardens, free and warm, the cold-day refuge.

Garden Free

Allan Gardens Conservatory

A 1910 Edwardian palm house — six greenhouses of tropical greenery under a glass dome, free and warm year-round.

Garden District Garden Free Victorian Glasshouse, Free

The centerpiece Palm House (1910) is a 16-sided glass-and-aluminum dome stuffed with towering palms; five connected greenhouses run from cacti to orchids. A small, free, deeply pleasant 30-45 minutes — and a refuge on a grey or freezing day.

North York (a Trek)

North York (a Trek) · 1 stop.

One pick, geographically flagged: the Aga Khan Museum, a 25-35 minute Uber north — arguably the most on-brand building in the city, but a dedicated half-day, not a downtown add-on.

Museum $20

Aga Khan Museum

A serene Fumihiko Maki-designed museum of Islamic art — the best building in Toronto almost no tourist sees, because it's a haul.

North York Museum $20 Worth the Trek

Geography flag: it's up at Don Mills and Eglinton, a 25-35 minute Uber from downtown — not a casual add-on. But Maki's luminous white-granite building in formal reflecting-pool gardens holds a world-class collection of Islamic manuscripts and ceramics. Make it a dedicated half-day or skip it.

Hours
Tue-Sun · closed Mon · free Wed 4-8

Day Ideas & Skips

Day Ideas, and What to Skip.

A few ready-made one-area outings, and the things you can skip.

The Bloor museum afternoon. ROM + Gardiner (across the street) + Bata Shoe Museum + a Yorkville stroll.

The west-side day. AGO + a Chinatown lunch + Kensington Market + Graffiti Alley.

The Old Town day. St. Lawrence Market + the Distillery District + the civic-architecture loop (Nathan Phillips Square, Osgoode Hall), with the Hockey Hall of Fame near Union.

The Casa Loma hill. Casa Loma + Spadina House next door — one Uber up.

Skip: the CN Tower 360 restaurant and the combo upsells (do the basic LookOut, eat at ground level), Ripley's Aquarium (a crowded pricey detour for a no-kids couple), and the Eaton Centre as a destination (it's a useful PATH waypoint, not a stop). The Aga Khan Museum is a dedicated half-day, not a downtown add-on.
