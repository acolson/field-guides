Where to Drink in Toronto, by Kind of Night.

Executive Summary

By the Numbers: a Cocktail-Heavy City.

Nine publications, dozens of bars. Toronto's bar scene is deeper than its restaurant scene at the top end — and overwhelmingly west of Spadina.

The methodology mirrors the Toronto eat section: pull every bar on a 2024–2025 critics' list, count overlaps, weight by frequency. Toronto's top tier is unusually strong — Bar Pompette was named Canada's #1 bar two years running and made the World's 50 Best Bars list globally; Bar Raval sits at #84 on the same list. Civil Works won North America's Best Cocktail Menu Award in 2025.

Twelve bars made Tier 1 — almost all of them cocktail rooms, with one hotel bar (Library Bar) and three late-night-anchor picks (Bowie, Bar Mordecai, Simpl Things). Tier 2 opens up to wine bars (Paradise Grapevine, Grey Gardens), the Ossington dive (Sweaty Betty's), a Japanese listening room (915 Dupont), and post-midnight standbys (416 Snack Bar). Wheat Sheaf Tavern — Toronto's oldest bar, open since 1849 and granted heritage protection in March 2025 — anchors the historic acknowledgment at the bottom.

Toronto's bar scene is overwhelmingly west of Spadina. Five top-15 picks sit on one block of College Street alone. Another four cluster on Dundas West. The Financial District has Library Bar — and that's it. Plan one downtown drink, three TTC-or-Uber expeditions west.
1

Bar Pompette is the must-do. Walk-in only, no reservations.

Canada's #1 bar, NA50Best #7, World's 50 Best globally. There's no reservation system by design — they want it to feel like a neighborhood bar. Arrive before 7pm or after 11pm to dodge the queue, or take the bar seats when they open.

2

The College Street block is the densest in the city.

Bar Pompette, Bar Raval, Slice of Life, Suite 115, and Lonely Diner are all within 400 feet of each other on College between Bathurst and Grace. Plan a single evening around the block — start with Suite 115 if Pompette's queue is unworkable, finish with Bar Raval's vermouth on tap.

3

Dundas West runs a parallel crawl.

Cry Baby Gallery, Bar Mordecai, and Mahjong Bar are three of the best cocktail rooms in the city and they're on the same six-block stretch. Add Bowie one block south on Ossington and you have a four-bar Saturday-night crawl.

4

Library Bar is the arrival drink.

Inside the Fairmont Royal York, across the street from Union Station. Named Canada's Best Hotel Bar in 2025. If you're getting off the train on Friday afternoon and want to start the trip with a cocktail, this is the one. Reserve a lunch table or walk in after 3pm.

Trip-Planning Notes

Four Practical Things to Know.

What changes when you're two travelers arriving by train Friday, no car, three nights in town.

A

Friday afternoon: walk to Library Bar from Union.

It's literally across the street from the train station — 100 Front Street West, Fairmont Royal York. James Grant's literary-themed cocktail program won Canada's Best Hotel Bar in 2025. Dress slightly up. The arrival drink writes itself.

B

Friday night: Ossington crawl ending at Bowie.

Start at Sweaty Betty's (the southern end of Ossington) for the dive warm-up, walk north to No Vacancy for the moody motel-inspired room, finish at Bowie — the NYT 36 Hours pick, hidden under a laundromat. Bowie takes walk-ins all night, so no anxiety about reservations.

C

Saturday: the College Street block, then dinner.

Late afternoon at Bar Raval for vermouth on tap (Saturday vermut hour is real), short walk to Bar Pompette if the queue is reasonable, fallback to Suite 115 or Slice of Life on the same block if not. Plan dinner in Little Italy (DaNico, Quetzal) so the bar crawl doesn't require Uber.

D

Sunday: Kensington wine and Grey Gardens.

If the trip needs a slower Sunday afternoon, walk through Kensington Market to Grey Gardens — Jen Agg's pastel wine bar, oyster menu, low-intervention list. Pair with one of the Tier 5 lunch picks (Nu Bügel is closed but Moonbean Coffee is the right warmup).

In Memoriam

Three bars that didn't make the list.

Worth noting for completeness — three Toronto bars that historically would have been on a guide like this, and aren't anymore.

Black Bull Tavern closed April 3, 2024 after 191 years on Queen Street West. Older than Confederation. The Queen West sidewalk's center of gravity for two centuries. Its closure was the kind of cultural loss that doesn't show up in restaurant journalism.

Pretty Ugly on Parkdale's Queen West stretch — a critics' darling for years — never reopened after COVID. The Cloak Bar in the King West speakeasy slot closed quietly in 2024. Both still appear on stale "best of Toronto" lists; both are gone.
