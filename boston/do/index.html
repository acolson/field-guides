What to Do in Boston, by Area and Era.

A mapped, area-organized guide to twenty-four things to do — leaning history, museums, and architecture, grouped so you can pair a couple of stops in one outing. Light on the outdoors, heavy on the walkable.

Sources: the museums & sites themselves · NPS Boston · Revolutionary Spaces · Meet Boston — hours and ticketing verified, but confirm seasonally.

How to Use This

Pick the Area, Then the Day.

Boston is small enough that the smart move is to do a couple of nearby things in one outing rather than zig-zag the city. This section is organized by area for exactly that — and skews to history, museums, and architecture over the outdoors.

24
things to do across 8 areas
9
free — incl. the BPL and Harvard Art Museums
3
Revolutionary clusters you can self-guide
2
I.M. Pei / Gehry architecture stops

The Freedom Trail is a painted line in the sidewalk designed to be walked solo — so this guide skips the paid costumed-guide tours and groups the Revolutionary sites by where they actually sit, so you can self-direct. Several of the best stops are free (the Boston Public Library, Faneuil Hall, the State House, and, newly, the Harvard Art Museums).

The museum heavyweights cluster too: the MFA and the Gardner share a Fenway block, the two Harvard museums share a Cambridge corner, and the modern-architecture pair (ICA, JFK Library) bookend the waterfront. Pick an area per outing and you'll never feel rushed.

The shape of it: one Fenway museum day, one self-guided Revolution walk, one Harvard Square morning — with Back Bay and Beacon Hill as the architecture in between. Skip the Duck Tours and the Cheers bar.
1

The Fenway museum afternoon is the marquee.

The MFA and the Isabella Stewart Gardner are directly across Evans Way. Do the MFA first (ration it to two wings), lunch, then the Gardner (smaller, you can finish it). Add the Mapparium on the walk back toward Back Bay.

2

Self-guide the Revolution, Beacon Hill to the North End.

State House → Granary Burying Ground → Old State House → Faneuil Hall → across the Greenway to the Paul Revere House → Old North Church, ending with a North End cannoli. A half-to-full day on foot, no tour required.

3

Copley is an architecture hour, free.

The Boston Public Library (Bates Hall + the Sargent murals) and Trinity Church (H.H. Richardson) face each other across the square, with the mirror-glass Hancock Tower reflecting both. Cap it with the Newbury Street / Comm Ave Mall walk.

4

Cambridge is a Red Line morning.

Harvard Art Museums (now free) and the Harvard Museum of Natural History (the Glass Flowers) are a 5-minute walk apart, with a Harvard Yard loop between. Kendall's MIT Museum + Gehry's Stata Center are a separate, more modern outing.

Fenway / Museum District

Fenway / Museum District · 4 stops.

The best museum cluster in the city: the MFA and the Gardner are directly across the street from each other, the Mapparium a short walk toward Back Bay, and Fenway Park around the corner. A full day on its own.

Museum $30

Museum of Fine Arts

New England's encyclopedic museum — the kind you ration rather than finish.

Fenway Museum $30 Sargent · Copley

Half a million objects and the strongest Sargent and Copley holdings anywhere. Pick two wings and a courtyard coffee; the Art of the Americas wing is the priority if you only have two hours.

Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum

A Venetian palazzo built around a flowering courtyard — with the empty frames from the unsolved 1990 heist still hanging.

Fenway Museum $22 The Heist Frames

Gardner's will froze the collection exactly as she arranged it, so it reads as one woman's vision. The glass-roofed courtyard is the heart; upstairs, the bare frames of the stolen Vermeer and Rembrandts are left as a deliberate absence.

The Mapparium

A three-story stained-glass globe you walk through on a glass bridge — the world as mapped in 1935.

Fenway Landmark $6 Walk Inside a Globe

Six hundred glass panels form a 30-foot inverted globe crossed by a glass footbridge, with the 1935 borders (Siam, French Indochina) all around you. The acoustics are the surprise: a perfect whisper-gallery sphere. Quick, quirky, unlike anything else in the city.

Fenway Park Tour

America's oldest active ballpark (1912) — the Green Monster, the lone red seat, the Living Museum.

Fenway Tour $25 1912 · Green Monster

Even for non-sports couples the hour-long tour reads as living history: the hand-operated Monster scoreboard, the red seat marking the longest homer, and 170,000 artifacts. Book ahead — game days sell out and the last tour leaves hours before first pitch.

Back Bay / Copley

Back Bay / Copley · 3 stops.

Architecture-rich and entirely walkable. Trinity Church and the Boston Public Library face each other across Copley Square; Newbury Street and the Commonwealth Avenue Mall are a block north. An easy half-day.

Architecture Free

Boston Public Library — McKim Building

A free Renaissance-palace library with a 218-foot barrel-vaulted reading room and a Sargent mural cycle most tourists walk past.

Copley Architecture Free Free · Sargent Murals

McKim's 1895 'palace for the people' is one of America's great public interiors, and entirely free. Bates Hall is the photograph everyone takes; upstairs, Sargent's unfinished Triumph of Religion murals get a fraction of the attention they deserve.

Trinity Church Boston

The 1877 church that gave the country a whole architectural style — Richardsonian Romanesque.

Copley Architecture $10 H.H. Richardson

H.H. Richardson's masterpiece, regularly ranked among the finest buildings in America, with a polychrome interior by John La Farge that's the reason to go inside. The mirror-glass Hancock Tower behind it reflects the church — an accidental architectural conversation.

Newbury St & Commonwealth Ave Mall

The most coherent Victorian streetscape in America — eight blocks of brownstone built on filled tidal flats in one generation.

Back Bay was filled in from the 1850s, which is why it's a perfect grid of uniform brownstone. Newbury Street is the shopping spine; one block north, the Commonwealth Avenue Mall is a Parisian tree-lined promenade — the loveliest urban walk in the city.

Beacon Hill

Beacon Hill · 2 stops.

The most photogenic walk in Boston, between the Common and Charles Street. Pairs with the State House at its eastern edge and the Athenaeum just downhill.

Walk Free

Beacon Hill Walk

Cobblestones, gas lamps, and the most-photographed lane in America — a 19th-century neighborhood that never modernized.

Beacon Hill Walk Free Acorn Street

Acorn Street is the postcard, but the whole hill rewards wandering: Louisburg Square (where Alcott lived), the purple windowpanes of a flawed 1820s glass batch turned status symbol, and Charles Street at the base for antiques and coffee. Wear flat shoes.

One of America's oldest independent libraries — usually members-only, but a day pass buys you the reading rooms.

Downtown / Freedom Trail

Downtown / Freedom Trail · 4 stops.

The dense Revolutionary cluster — these sites are within a 15-minute walk of one another and string together as a self-guided morning. No paid tour required.

Architecture Free

Massachusetts State House

Charles Bulfinch's 1798 capitol with the gold-leafed dome — and a free docent tour inside.

Downtown Architecture Free Free · Bulfinch Dome

Bulfinch's red-brick capitol topped by a dome originally sheathed in copper by Paul Revere. The free tour gets you into Doric Hall and the Senate chamber; even from outside, the facade and the Shaw/54th Regiment Memorial earn the stop.

Historic Free

Granary Burying Ground

The most consequential graveyard in America — Revere, Hancock, Sam Adams, and the Boston Massacre victims, on one block.

Downtown Historic Free Revere · Hancock · Adams

Established 1660: three signers of the Declaration, Paul Revere, and the five killed in the Massacre. Most slate stones were rearranged into tidy rows over the centuries, so the markers don't always sit over the graves — a small detail about how the city curated its memory.

Boston's oldest surviving public building, where the Declaration was first read to the city — above the cobblestone circle marking the Massacre. Run by Revolutionary Spaces; a single joint ticket also covers Old South.

North End

North End · 3 stops.

Boston's oldest neighborhood and the Freedom Trail's most charming stretch: two Revolutionary sites plus Italian-Boston street life, all on foot from Faneuil Hall.

Paul Revere House

The c.1680 home of Paul Revere — the oldest building in downtown Boston.

North End Historic $6 Oldest House Downtown

A genuine 17th-century wooden house in a quiet North End courtyard, furnished to evoke the Revere family. Small and self-guided — 20 minutes, not an hour — but it's the real thing, with the 900-pound Revere bell in the courtyard.

'One if by land, two if by sea' — the 1723 steeple where the lanterns hung, and Boston's oldest church.

The signal-lantern church of the Longfellow poem, with a beautiful Georgian interior of white box pews. The Discovery Pass adds the crypt and an immersive exhibit; the surrounding Old North campus makes it a longer stop than you'd expect.

Charlestown

Charlestown · 2 stops.

Across the harbor, the end of the Trail. The Navy Yard and Bunker Hill are a 15-minute walk apart; take the Long Wharf ferry over for the harbor view.

Historic Free

USS Constitution & Museum

The world's oldest commissioned warship still afloat — and you can walk her decks, free.

Charlestown Historic Free Old Ironsides

'Old Ironsides,' launched 1797, undefeated, still crewed by active-duty sailors who give the tours. Boarding is free (bring photo ID); the adjacent museum is donation-based and tells the fuller story. Together, the best hour in Charlestown.

Bunker Hill Monument

A 221-foot granite obelisk over the first major battle of the Revolution — climb 294 steps for the view.

Charlestown Landmark Free 294 Steps

The 1843 obelisk marks the June 1775 battle (actually on Breed's Hill). The climb is 294 steps with no elevator; the payoff is a harbor panorama. Free timed climb passes come from the Lodge at the base. Skippable if stairs aren't your thing.

Cambridge (Harvard & MIT)

Cambridge (Harvard & MIT) · 4 stops.

A Red Line ride across the river. Harvard Square's museums plus the Yard are one outing; the MIT Museum and Gehry's Stata Center in Kendall are a separate, more modern one.

Harvard Art Museums

Three museums under one Renzo Piano glass roof — and, as of recently, free to everyone, every day.

The Fogg, Busch-Reisinger, and Sackler collections unified under Piano's luminous 2014 glass-roofed atrium — the building is the first thing worth seeing. Strong Bauhaus and German Expressionism; admission is now free to all.

Seaport & Columbia Point

Seaport & Columbia Point · 2 stops.

The modern-architecture corner. The ICA's cantilevered glass box anchors the Seaport; the JFK Library's I.M. Pei tower is a short ride south — both about the building as much as the contents.

Museum $20

Institute of Contemporary Art

A cantilevered glass box hanging over Boston Harbor — the city's contemporary-art counterweight to the MFA.

Seaport Museum $20 Free Thursday Nights

Diller Scofidio + Renfro's 2006 building cantilevers a glass gallery over the water, with a glass-floored Mediatheque framing nothing but the harbor below. The collection is rotating contemporary; the building and setting earn the stop. Free Thursday evenings.

JFK Presidential Library & Museum

I.M. Pei's stark white tower on a windswept harbor point — the Kennedy story inside a piece of architecture.

Columbia Point Museum $20 I.M. Pei

Pei's 1979 building is the reason architecture people make the trip: a sharp white tower culminating in a vast glass pavilion with the harbor and a single flag. The exhibits walk 1960, the Cuban Missile Crisis, and the space race. Plan it as its own half-day.

Day Ideas & Skips

Day Ideas, and What to Skip.

A few ready-made one-area outings, and the things you can skip.

The Fenway museum day. MFA + Isabella Stewart Gardner (across the street) + the Mapparium on the way back toward Back Bay.

The self-guided Revolution. Beacon Hill → State House → Granary → Old State House → Faneuil Hall → Paul Revere House → Old North → a North End cannoli.

The Copley architecture hour. Boston Public Library + Trinity Church + a Newbury Street / Comm Ave Mall walk.

The Harvard Square morning. Harvard Art Museums + Harvard Museum of Natural History + a Harvard Yard loop.

Skip: the paid Freedom Trail walking tour (self-guide it — it's a line in the sidewalk), the Cheers bar (it's the exterior; the inside looks nothing like the show), the Quincy Market food court (look, don't eat — the North End is better), and the Duck Tours (fun but corny; you'd rather linger).
