Boston, A Long Weekend.

The Trip at a Glance

The Shape of It

Boston is small enough to walk, dense enough to feel like a real city. Three to five days lets you eat well, see the museums, do the Freedom Trail without rushing, and have one night out that isn't planned around tomorrow morning. Stay near the Common and you can hit most neighborhoods on foot.

Where to Base Yourself

The right hotel neighborhood is South End, Back Bay, or right on the Common. From any of those, the North End is a 15-minute walk, the Seaport is a quick Lyft, and Fenway is one stop on the T. The hotels guide goes deeper on which buildings to actually book.

Don't Miss

An Italian sub from Monica's Mercato, a dinner at one of the Tier 1 restaurants (Sarma, Neptune, or Toro — pick by mood), a walk through the North End, and one museum afternoon. Everything else is flex.

What This Guide Is

Editorial picks, not an exhaustive list. All four sections are built – 107 places researched across seven hotel signals, ten restaurant/bar publications, and the museums and historic sites themselves. Read the section you need, when you need it.

About This Guide

Boston rewards travelers who organize around neighborhood and time of day rather than ticking off a checklist. The North End at dinner is a different city from the North End at breakfast; the Seaport on a weekday afternoon is not the Seaport on a Saturday night.

This guide is structured to match how the trip actually moves. The restaurants section is tiered by critical consensus — Tier 1 is what every publication agrees on, Tier 5 is breakfast and lunch organized by where you'll be that day. Use the section you need; the sections are independent.

Researched against the New York Times, Eater Boston, Boston Magazine, Condé Nast Traveler, Infatuation, Resy Hit List, and the Boston Globe's Best of the Best.
